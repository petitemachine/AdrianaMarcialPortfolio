UX STUDY CASE




MAYNOOTH FURNITURE

Project
Maynooth Furniture wanted to redefine and connect with their target audience. Through research, we identified their core persona: a woman in her 60s with grown-up children, a love for interior design, and a desire to refresh her home. My role was to design a new digital experience tailored to her needs — and to validate it with real users who matched that profile.

Objective
The company came to me with a clear persona but no additional design assets or direction — they gave me full creative freedom to make a proposal. My biggest challenge was also my biggest opportunity: thinking like my persona at every step.

Key questions I kept asking throughout the process: Is she likely to wear glasses? Would the font size feel comfortable to her? Are the buttons large enough? Do the colours feel familiar and trustworthy for her age group? The brief didn't mention any specific disability, but I made a conscious decision to design with the most common age-related considerations in mind — visual accessibility, clear hierarchy, and effortless navigation. The intended outcome was a website that felt welcoming and easy to use from the very first visit.

Process
I worked mostly independently, building and refining a poll distributed through websites and social channels. Our participants were women of a similar age and lifestyle to the persona — many of them nearly retired, with a strong interest in home interiors. Their response was genuinely encouraging: people were excited to see more from Maynooth Furniture, even at the prototype stage.

Testing was structured in three stages. In the first, we introduced the company. If a participant already knew the brand, they were redirected to a separate questionnaire. In the final stage, participants completed a task on the prototype without any instructions — a deliberate choice to measure intuitive navigation. I iterated on the poll and the design based on the data, adjusting wherever friction appeared, until users could complete every task independently.

I maintained constant communication with Maynooth throughout. One of the strengths of working with them was their well-organised, accessible brand information — it made iteration fast. When team support was available, I embraced it; the team was versatile and enthusiastic about the project.

Results
All goals were achieved within the planned timeline. Poll and questionnaire responses were positive, and participants from our target demographic engaged enthusiastically with both the research and the prototype. The data confirmed that the design direction resonated — users responded well to the colour palette, the style, and the navigation structure.

Reflection
This project reminded me that good design starts with genuine listening. My biggest takeaway was to treat every comment, data point, and perspective as valuable input. Making decisions grounded in both qualitative feedback and quantitative data gave me confidence in each iteration, and the final outcome was something the whole team was proud of.
